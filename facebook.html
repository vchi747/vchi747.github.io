Improving Mental Health in Facebook

Data-Driven UX/Product Design - UCSD - October to December 2020

Background

The design prompt that we decided to pursue is prompt three-- maintaining personal connections. We specifically want to focus on reducing online clutter and noise to improve one’s mental health. The personal connection app that we wanted to tackle was Facebook. Facebook has a large user base that targets a very extensive market. We want to add more features to the app that will allow for a better experience in the app. Through crowd sourcing and internal NLP algorithms, we aim to label content into categories. Sorting and labelling content allows users to control the volume of information displayed and creates a more controlled environment for what ultimately reaches them. Our improved design solution is motivated by a trend of declining mental health as users of social media applications are spending increasing amounts of time aimlessly scrolling through content that is generated on their feeds. Thus, in an effort to provide the option of feed management, we wanted to tackle this problem area for the benefit of users who need a boost in their productivity or a means to control their information input. A byproduct benefit for our secondary user, Facebook, is an improvement in the existing manual filtering option for individual posts so that Facebook can generate more relevant content and ad recommendations for their users.

Problem Statement

Social media enthusiasts who are averaging 3-4 hours on Facebook daily need an improved design of the current content filtering feature on the application to better personalize their feeds with content they want to see and to reduce irrelevant information overload.

Research Methods

Our goal is to discover a specific need for users to filter content on their social media feeds to give them the opportunity to receive personalized content recommendations. We hope to gain varying personal experiences and perspectives on how our topic can improve the daily lives of Facebook users, specifically in the midst of this global pandemic.

In order to answer our problem statement, we have decided to use surveys and interviews to grasp a better understanding of our target population. Surveys provide us with a more standardized way to compare statistical data from individual responses. It also allows the participants to give their own personal feedback on the shortcomings of the social media website. That would allow us, the designers, to better understand the exact problems and shortcomings of the current filters implemented by Facebook. The second research method that we are implementing is interviews. Interviews give us accurate accounts of personal use and help either support or reject the statistical data that we have gathered from our surveys. This provides a means to double check if the data we gathered from our surveys accurately reflects our target population.

From our initial surveys, we gathered 37 responses from members of our immediate community composed of our friends, coworkers, and mutual friends. We opted for safely contacting and surveying our immediate networks during these unprecedented social distancing circumstances, which explains the limited reach of our survey. Moreover, we aim to conduct one-on-one interviews with 3 to 5 individuals from our target audience of social media enthusiasts. The Facebook users that we have surveyed thus far are relevant to our design prompt since they come from varying demographic backgrounds, interests, and reasons for Facebook use, but there is a relation in the amount of time they spend on the application. We aim to draw connections from similarities among the problems that these daily Facebook users and social media enthusiasts experience.

Research Findings

In order to get an assessment of public attitudes towards the current state of Facebook, we used surveys to gather information. We will be breaking down the information gathered from our surveys.

In our surveys, we primarily utilized two methods to gauge user sentiment towards Facebook. We used the Likert scale and the nominal scale. The Likert scale was used in our survey to give more freedom to the participants in conveying their feelings, choosing from a range of values. It informed us about the kind of people who are using Facebook. With our Likert scale question, we noticed that our data was heavily left-skewed. The “previous question” mentioned in the bar graph below refers to our question asking, “How much time on average do you spend on Facebook everyday?”. From the left to right, it reads “I spend too much time on Facebook” to “ I am satisfied with the time I spend on Facebook”. This was used to gauge whether users even have a desire to use Facebook more or less, and from our findings we see that the majority feel like they use the application just the right amount.

Time Spend on Facebook(1 is little and 5 is alot)

In order to dive deeper into our problem scope we decided to observe users’ reactions and feelings towards the content popping up in their news feeds. To our surprise, a substantial number of people were not completely dissatisfied with what they currently see on Facebook.

This brings up one of the strongest findings in our survey: Around 20 percent of our participants are not happy with what they see on Facebook, responding with “Not completely” and “No”. This percentage was shocking to us because we constantly hear how people dislike Facebook’s recommendations and posts in passing conversations. Combining this statistic with our follow-up question, we get a better understanding of our participants. It leads into another interesting finding to be deduced.

In our follow-up question we asked if users know about Facebook’s built in filter to remove content. Around 75% of our respondents knew of or used this filter while using the app.

Combining the data from this and the previous question’s data, we can draw a notable connection. Since users mostly like the content they see and are using the built in filter on Facebook, it proves that filters do improve user experience, but it can be improved to encompass more individuals that do not yet know of its existence.

Personal feedback from participants provided significant information to us about specific wishes and desires because it allowed them to give personal accounts of their experiences and preferences. We found some common feedback amongst all answers we received:

1. The first one that we found to be very prevalent were responses that mentioned political posts. We see that political views either drive people or deter people from using Facebook more.

2. The next frequent responses were ones related to ads. Ads are one of Facebook’s main sources of revenue. Removing them would destroy the company, so we took this input to consider making an improved filter that aligns with personal interests more accurately, promoting positive feedback to ads without removing them altogether.

From these personal responses from our recipients, we noticed a unifying theme, a desire to have greater control of content that they see. Facebook already has filters in-place but they are preset by the internal algorithms, leaving the user with minimal control in determining and managing the content they want to see.

To summarize our findings, around 20 percent of our participants do not like the current state of their news feed. Moreover, many of our participants currently use Facebook’s filters to improve their content, but they still are dissatisfied with the current state of Facebook’s filters.

Competitive Audit

Insights
The three applications that we researched in our competitive analysis helped form a better understanding of existing filtering and content-recommending solutions. We observed that while most of the popular social media applications tackle the problem of hiding individual irrelevant user profiles or posts, they do not allow users to filter their feeds based on the specific categories that we want to tackle-- e.g., mood, recent, content.

Flow Chart

For this UI flow, we decided the Home screen to be the entry point because that is what the user first sees when they log into the website/app. The flow is split into three different options the user can choose in order to improve their experience. The main three things that they can manipulate are the posts, advertisements, and user settings. Each of the listed things access different screens that allow the user to customize their account. For the Post and Advertisements, each have their ability to completely remove what they see but then we added our classification feature as a new screen that users can go into. The Classify Post screen would allow users to classify the post based on the Genre, Mood, and if it's popular or not. The third option from the Home screen would be the user settings. The user can enter and change options about their Timeline and Ads. This goes to the heart of trying to fix our problem, which is to filter out unnecessary noise from posts.

From iterating on a multitude of hand-drawn sketches, we learned that there can be multiple variations of a screen that can impact the way a user interacts with a feature. The interaction with an interface can affect the complexity of performing the function itself. Thus, we strived to make it as intuitive as possible.

Wireframe

The low-fidelity prototypes above demonstrate an interface option for our design extension of Facebook’s content filtering feature.The Wireframe is an iteration of our UX Flow, which focuses on using the existing means of directly managing individual posts to access the content filtering feature while also adding it as an option within the user’s settings. This wireframe adds nested pages and drop-down menus for Facebook’s current icons for accessing settings. This option allows the users to easily access the filter settings from the user’s profile settings as well as individual posts and advertisements. This wireframe allows users to manually categorize posts in real-time as they appear on their news feed. It offers multiple entry points from the home page that are seamlessly integrated into existing menu buttons and settings.It offers a solution for our problem statement by providing users with methods to reduce information overload and personalize their news feeds.

Final Design

These design decisions were directly informed by our user tests on our low-fidelity prototypes. One of the users we interviewed shared this about the filter icon to create new filters for the news feed, “The top buttons are very intuitive. I know I either click on the funnel cone for filter, search button, and messenger. Those are nice to have at the top since I probably [would] use those most”. They went on to describe the structure of the content filtering page from our low-fidelity prototype saying, “I like how the recent filters are at the top. It’s helpful given that people typically search the same things and if they want to they can access new filters but the recent ones are at your disposal”. We used these insights to model our high-fidelity prototype from this element of our low-fidelity one.

Users who frequent social media applications want to feel connected with others and this is something our design extension hopes to achieve by considering feedback from our low-fidelity prototype and implementing them into our 2 entry points shown in our high-fidelity prototype above.
